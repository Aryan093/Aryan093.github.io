Haptophytes include phytoplankton that possess a unique structure called a haptonema. Coccolithophores are a subgroup of haptophytes.

Haptophytes are a group of unicellular microorganisms commonly found in marine ecosystems. These microscopic algae are recognized for the presence of a unique structure called a "haptonema," which is a fine filament used for capturing prey and assisting in movement. Haptophytes are photosynthetic organisms that contain pigments such as chlorophyll and carotenoids, allowing them to harness sunlight for energy. They contribute to primary production in marine food webs and play a role in carbon cycling in the oceans. One well-known subgroup of haptophytes is coccolithophores, which are characterized by their calcium carbonate plates or coccoliths. Coccolithophores have a significant impact on the global carbon cycle as they sequester carbon dioxide and play a role in regulating ocean acidity. Additionally, they are vital components of marine ecosystems, influencing marine food webs and ecosystem dynamics.
